Rise of Online Commerce: Amazon and eBay

Amazon

In the 1990s, Amazon and eBay were launched on the Internet, helping introduce the revolution of e-commerce. Jeff Bezos first launched Amazon in 1995. When thinking about the web and business, he made a list of products that would be ideal to sell with books being at the top of each list. From his perspective, books were the number one thing that would be worth selling online. Within months of Amazon’s launch, Amazon was shipping books to 45 countries and all 50 of the United States. Amazon found their business was growing greatly. Bezos even claimed it to be Earth’s Biggest Bookstore.

eBay

That same year, Pierre Omidyar was a software engineer who had the idea of an online auction site that would eventually become eBay. He set out to create a more fair and efficient marketplace where the playing field was even among all consumers. Anyone could auction off anything they desired online and anyone could also bid on those same products. Soon after eBay was launched online, the company was earning thousands of dollars in fees. What became surprisingly popular on the site was the auctioning of collectibles, and even specifically beanie babies.

Success of the Internet: Moore's Law and Metcalfe's Law

Essentially, the Internet became even more successful and there were more users than ever. Amazon and eBay were also very successful companies. Overall it only took the Internet 4 years for there to be over 50 million users. Part of this is in thanks to Moore’s Law and the silicon chip. Moore’s Law is a statement that claims that the speed and power of integrated circuits will double every 18 months, aka growing exponentially over time. Transistors became smaller and smaller and allowed for faster and more efficient technology. Once Moore’s Law was seen to be true, it continued to hold true over the years. Additionally, Metcalfe’s Law also came into play. What Metcalfe’s Law is saying is that the number of users is proportional to the number of connections in the system. The more user there are, the larger the network becomes. And the larger the network becomes, the more powerful and useful it can be.

Encryption

When it comes to online commerce, there are also big implications for security. Therefore, encryption and public-key cryptography were created. Encryption is the process of encoding information such as credit card numbers or addresses in online transactions. Public-key cryptography on the other hand is a form of encryption that uses public keys and private keys. Public keys are generally available to anyone to encrypt certain data but must be decrypted by a private key. Similarly, private keys can encrypt some data, but then it must be decrypted by a public key. This helps prevent any data or information from being hacked by anyone who shouldn’t have access to data other than those whom it was intended to.

The Dot Com Bubble

When Amazon and eBay had their IPOs (initial public offerings) in the late 1990s, this started the mania that is the Dot Com Bubble. Any company with a “dot com” in its company name or was otherwise known as an online company boomed in the stock market. Many shares were bought these companies made much money from it. E-commerce was taking off and the 90s was becoming the e-decade. By 1999, there were 250 Internet IPOs. However, in 2000 on Black Friday the NASDAQ index fell by more than 25% and later on with the September 11 attacks on the Twin Towers, that number continued to drop. Many Internet companies had to shut down and other big companies that did survive still hurt from the burst of the Dot Com Bubble.

The Bubble Bursts

After the burst of the Dot Com Bubble, Wall Street was felt to blame. The Attorney General of New York at the time set to reform the IPO industry. Henry Blodgett, a former Wall Street analyst, was charged with civil securities fraud based on critical emails about how he figured not many Internet companies would survive for long and would suffer from a stock market crash. Because he knew that the Dot Com Bubble was a speculative bubble that would eventually pop, he ended up being banned from the securities industry for life.
